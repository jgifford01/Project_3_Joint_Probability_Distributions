Part I - Univariate Probability Density Functions¶

First, a review of univariate probability density function. A probability density function (PDF) describes how likely it is to observe some outcome resulting from a data-generating process.

Let $X$ be a random variable. The probability density function takes the form of $f_X(x)$ in which two conditions must be satisfied:

  1. $f_X(x) \geq 0$
  2. $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}f(x) dx = 1$

The probability of observing values between $a$ and $b$ is

$P(X\in [a,b]) =\int_{a}^{b}f_X(x) dx$.

The value of the function $f_X(x)$ represents the probability of a given $x$ within an infinitely small region.

Consider a random sample from a standard-normal population distribution, in which the binwidth is 1.

At the interval $x=[-0.5,0.5]$, the sample proportion is roughly $\frac{4,000}{10,000}$. It is clear to see how we arrive at a probabilitity density of $0.4$ when $\Delta x=1$. In other words; the product of density $(0.4)$ and the interval of $x=1$ yiels a probability proportion of $0.4$.

The probability on an interval of $X$ is equal to the density times that interval, or the binwidth. More succinctly,

$\displaystyle {\sum_{i=1}^n} f(x_i) \Delta x$

In which for this example,

$\displaystyle {\sum_{i=1}^7} f(x) *(1) \approx 1$

where $f(x)$ is our discrete probability density.

As the binwidth approaches $0$, we find the approximation achieves more accuracy, i.e., closer to $1$.

The expected value of $X$ is defined as

$E[X] = \displaystyle {\lim_{n\rightarrow \infty}}\sum_{i=1}^n x_i f(x_i) \Delta x = \int_{a}^{b} x f(x) dx $.

The intuition behind this is that we are summing up the products of each $x$ value with their corresponding probability slice, $f(x) dx$.

To get a better understanding of expected values, we will run a simulation of 10 dice being randomly rolled, then summed over 100,000 iterations.

Part II - Joint Probability Density Function, Covariance, and Correlation¶

Similar to a univariate PDF, A joint probability density function describes how likely it is to simultaneously observe two or more outcomes resulting from data-generating processes. Formally, we write this as $P(X \cap Y)$.

Let $X,Y \in \mathbb{R}$ be random variables. The probability density function takes the form of $f(x,y)$ in which two conditions must be satisfied:

  1. $f(x,y) \geq 0$
  2. $\int \int_{R} f(x,y) dx dy = 1$

The probability of observing values between $x=[a,b]$ and $y=[c,d]$ is

$P(X\in [a,b],Y \in [c,d]) =\int_{c}^{d}\int_{a}^{b}f(x,y) dx dy$.

The value of the function $f(x,y)$ represents the probability of a given $x$ and $y$ occuring within an infinitely small region.

Compare a random sample from a standard-normal population distribution, in which the binwidths are decreasing.

We find that these plots follow a similar mechanism to the univariate distributions, but with another dimension added! The joint probability is simply the product of the joint density value and its corresponding, infinitely small area $dA = dx dy$. Furthermore,

$\displaystyle {\sum_{i=1}^n} f(x,y) \Delta A$.

Expected Value of a Joint Distribution & Covariance¶

The expectation value of the joint distribution of random variables $x$ and $y$ can be written as

$E[g(X,Y)] = \int_{c}^{d}\int_{a}^{b} g(x,y) \cdot p(x,y) dxdy$.

The intuition carries through from above, with simply another dimension added. To help myself think about this visually, we are adding up samll neighborhoods of density times area and multiplying it by the value of $g(x,y)$ at that point. This gives us in a sense the average value of $g(x,y)$.
